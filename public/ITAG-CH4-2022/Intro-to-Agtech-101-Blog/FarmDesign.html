My Planning Process

The process of developing a plan for your indoor farm system can seem intimidating when you're unsure where to even start. A good first step is to narrow down your growing goals by determining which of the many benefits of starting an indoor smart farm system are most important to you. I determined that the top priorities of my farm build were:


Getting exposure to sustainable urban agriculture and indoor farming (with a long-term goal of eventually becoming completely self-sufficient with food)
Having access to a low-maintenance, cost-saving nutritional resource for myself and my community
Gaining hands-on experience integrating agriculture and technology
Improving my overall health and personal well-being through horticulture

Below is a sketch map of what I envisioned my operation to look like:


For the farm build, I decided to go with what I deemed the most beginner friendly out of all the hydroponic systems we had covered: Deep Water Culture (aka DWC).

My design consists of two DWC buckets, a seed germination dome, and grow lights which I've set up on an adjustable wire rack. The adjustable height gives me room to accommodate my crops when they grow taller and need more room.


Crops and Harvest Time


For my first hydroponic harvest, I chose to grow peppers and tomatoes since I had learned in our first agriculture lesson on crop selection that these are two crops that tend to grow well with hydroponics. To further ensure successful results I decided to choose variations of these crops that grow smaller than most you would find at a grocery store.

Conducting Needs Assessment



Once I had the details of my farm system figured out i.e. which crops I was growing and which hydroponic system I would build, I could begin compiling my list of materials. In order to figure out which supplies I would require I had to assess my space and make note of any potential risk factors or obstacles that might lead to an unsuccessful system. The following are the risk factors I knew I would need to consider:


1.) Electrical Hazards

It's important to practice proper electrical safety with any electrical home appliances in order to ensure both personal and public safety- especially when you're adding water to the equation. The last thing anyone wants is to risk serious injuries from something as easily avoidable as a poor installation. Avoiding damaged or worn cords and open wiring mitigates the risk of electrocution, I wouldn't have to worry too much about this since all the electrical appliances I planned on using were arriving brand new and in good shape. Another good practice is to organize your cords neatly. This is something that requires playing around with as it depends on the layout of your system, length of the cables and how far everything is from the nearest outlet. For both safety and aesthetic purposes (and my sanity) I wanted to avoid having a tangled mess of wires and I did so by tying my cables together using string (repurposed straps of used covid masks) and concealing them by aligning them neatly along the wire metal rack I was using to hold all of my equipment.
